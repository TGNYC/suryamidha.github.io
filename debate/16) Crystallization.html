16) Crystallization

Topic

How to win around when you're loosing

Lecture

  • Why crystallization is important
    • Judges are human and are inclined to vote for the better debater
    • The flow can get really messy, will vote the way you tell them
    • Judges don't pay as much attention, will vote off what you say
  • Tips
    • Know your judge
      • They'll tell you what they don't like
      • If you're good at crystalizing, they can be wrong in your favor
      • Be perceptually dominant
        • Which side is more true
    • Use prep wisely
    • Think about issue selection (15 seconds)
      • Go for most offensive arguments you have
      • Have a master plan, focus on the end game
      • If you win an argument, you win the debate
      • Only need to win those arguments
      • Talk about your own arguments, talk about why their's are unimportant
      • Quality over quantity
    • Overview
      • First 15 seconds
        • What's going to happen in the speech
        • A story
        • How the judge is going to vote
      • Last 30, 40 seconds
        • What happened
        • Explain the arguments one more time
        • Flushing out the impacts
        • Strategic Implications
      • Valid story
        • Pretend to write an RFD from the judge's perspective
    • Persuasion
      • Have two different voices
        • Slow down for overview
        • Spreading for line by line
      • Avoid argument personification
        • Explain the argument
        • Avoid basically or essentially
        • Use "I'm winning" instead of "I'm essentially winning"
        • Phrase your arguments as offensively as possible
        • Instead of "No Warrant", "No shred of credibility"
        • "This is the brink, and we must ask now"
        • "This is game over"
        • End of the world, the only way you can vote is one side
    • Good Extensions
      • How the argument has developed and its strategic implication
      • How your arguments take out the negative case
      • Go into round with prewritten extensions
      • Make impacts off the top of your head
      • Indict your opponent's extensions
        • No impact to their extensions
      • Resolves messy extensions by discussing specific thresholds
        • What it means to win each contention
        • Meta Weighing
          • Porability is more important than magnitude
        • Say you have better arguments
      • Think about the round how the judge's thinking about the round
    • Always look up
      • When explaining important arguments, or impacts look up to the judge
      • Syncing when you look up for when you're speaking slowly and when you're talking fast when looking down
    • Confidence
      • Give a really good 2AR
      • Explain why you're arguments are true
    • Know when to Concede
      • If you don't need to win an argument, don't waste time
      • Explain why argument is irrelevant
      • Admit when rounds are very messy
      • Summarize what happens at the end
      • Tell them how to vote
    • Don't overdo dropped arguments
      • Look at how arguments interact
      • Be good about issue selection
    • Predict your opponent
      • Address arguments you know other side will collapse to
      • Spend time on one contention you know they're going to talk about
    • Use layers
      • Even if scenarios
      • Use them as much as possible
    • Convince the judge you won
    • "This round boils down to [framework/card]"
    • The only way to vote AFF is [], but []
    • Your own args line by line
    • Summarize
    • Write RFDs for the other person!
